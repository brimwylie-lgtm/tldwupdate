Insight #3: Sensemaking is Your Hidden Superpower

One of the most undervalued capabilities in corporate communications is what Professor Deborah Ancona at MIT calls “sensemaking” — the ability to make sense of a complex environment in order to work on it or work with it more effectively. And here is the provocative truth: there is no business function more adept at sensemaking and sense-giving than the communications function.

While risk teams build heat maps and business continuity plans focus on technical recovery, communicators are uniquely positioned to synthesize complexity, identify patterns across seemingly unrelated events, and translate strategic intent into actionable narrative. In a world of permacrisis and polycrisis, this ability to create clarity from chaos is not just valuable. It is existential.

Insight #7: Character Scandals Hit Harder Than Capability Crises

Research from Sentient Decision Science analyzing 440 crises across 40 markets over 30 years reveals a striking pattern: the greatest long-term damage to share price comes from what they call “white collar crime and mismanagement.” In other words, character failures. The relatively lowest impact? Cyber hacks and operational disruptions.

People price in operational capability crises. They do not forgive character-led scandals. And yet most crisis preparedness focuses almost entirely on operational scenarios while treating values and culture as abstract concepts rather than material risk factors.

Thought Leadership Post #1

Rod Cartwright
Crisis & Reputation Expert • 4d

I have spent 30 years in the crisis communications space.

And I am increasingly convinced that we have been underselling our most valuable capability.

Not message development. Not media training. Not reputation monitoring.

Sensemaking.

The ability to take a complex, uncertain, rapidly evolving situation and create clarity. To synthesize signal from noise. To identify patterns across seemingly unrelated events. To translate confusion into actionable understanding.

This is what communicators do better than any other function in the organization.

Risk teams build heat maps. Business continuity plans focus on technical recovery. Legal teams assess liability. Operations teams fix what broke.

But when the storm hits, who makes sense of what it all means? Who translates that understanding into narrative? Who connects internal confusion to external stakeholder needs?

The communications function.

And yet most organizations position communicators as storytellers, not sensemakers. As messengers, not strategic advisors. As the team that polishes the message after the decisions have been made.

That is a fundamental misunderstanding of the role.

In a world of permacrisis and polycrisis, sensemaking is not a nice-to-have communication skill. It is a core strategic capability.

So here is my challenge to every CCO and communications leader: Stop positioning yourself as the storyteller-in-chief. Start positioning yourself as the sensemaker-in-chief.

Thought Leadership Post #2
RP
Rachel Phillips
Director, Corporate Reputation at Ipsos • 1w

If I were made prime minister (and luckily for all of you that will never happen), I would ban the term “PR crisis.”

Here is why.

Most of what organizations call a PR crisis is not a communications problem at all. It is either an operational failure (capability) or a values failure (character).

And the distinction matters more than most people realize.

Capability crises are about technical competence. A factory explodes. A supply chain fails. A product is recalled. These are operational breakdowns that can be fixed with better systems and processes.

Character crises are about values, ethics, culture, and behavior. A CEO lies. A company covers up safety violations. These are moral failures that reveal something fundamental about who you are.

And here is the uncomfortable truth: character scandals do far more long-term damage than capability crises.

Research analyzing 440 crises over 30 years found that the greatest share price impact came from white collar crime and mismanagement. The lowest impact? Cyber hacks.

People price in operational failures. They do not forgive character scandals.

And yet when I work with organizations on crisis preparedness, 90% of the scenarios they want to war-game are operational. Almost no one wants to scenario-plan around a senior executive behaving unethically.

Why? Because those conversations are uncomfortable.

But ignoring character risks does not make them go away. It just means you are unprepared when they materialize.

That is where the real reputational risk lives.

Why Most Organizations Are Preparing for the Wrong Kind of Crisis

By Rachel Phillips & Rod Cartwright • 9 min read

Every year, organizations across the globe invest millions in crisis preparedness. They build crisis communications plans. They create risk heat maps. They establish business continuity protocols.

And yet when the crisis hits, many still fail spectacularly. Why? Because most crisis preparedness programs are built on a fundamental misunderstanding of what a crisis actually is.

The Etymology of Crisis

The word “crisis” comes from the Greek meaning “a moment where a choice, a decision, or a judgment has to be made.” It describes a turning point, not an endpoint. And yet most people approach crisis as disaster, catastrophe, panic.

The Empirical Evidence

Research from Oxford Metrica analyzing 200 mass fatality events found that companies who responded badly saw share price fall by an average of 15% over six months. But companies that responded well saw a net uptick of 5% over the same period.

The Real Risk: Character, Not Capability
